\begin{abstract}
Irreversibility appears across physical, computational, and cognitive systems, but is usually explained only inside domain-specific formalisms. This paper develops a substrate-independent structural account of irreversible transition. A system is modeled by a state space $S$, a coherence functional $C$, a contradiction functional $\chi$, and a threshold $\theta$. The control variable is the tension
\[
T(s)=\chi(s)-C(s).
\]
The central claim is that when tension exceeds threshold, admissible dynamics can require a non-invertible reorganization that discards identity-relevant structure. We formalize identity as an attractor-basin equivalence class, define the Gate as a threshold-triggered reorganization operator, and prove a point-of-no-return theorem: once identity-relevant structure has been removed and is not recoverable from the post-transition state, no admissible forward trajectory can return the system to its pre-transition identity. We then derive a positive lower bound on reconstruction error under basin separation, prove monotonic tension descent for Gate-selected updates, and show that an exit-condition ratio law implies eventual Gate crossing under bounded coherence. Empirically, we integrate the provided neural plots directly into the manuscript and show a consistent three-regime pattern---integration, criticality, and collapse---under increasing contradiction. We also include a fully specified cognitive pilot protocol that operationalizes the same structure in human interpretive transitions. The resulting framework yields theorem-level support, computational evidence, and a testable human methods program within a single unified submission.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Irreversible change is central to thermodynamics, measurement-like updates, continual learning, and human reinterpretation. Yet the mechanism of one-way transition is usually treated as local to each domain: entropy increase in one setting, catastrophic forgetting in another, collapse-like update somewhere else. This paper advances a simpler and more general proposition: what makes a transition irreversible is not the substrate but the structural loss of recoverable identity-relevant information under constraint violation.

The framework has four components. First, a system carries coherence $C$ and contradiction $\chi$. Second, the net structural pressure is the tension $T=\chi-C$. Third, when $T$ exceeds a threshold $\theta$, ordinary evolution may be replaced by a reorganization operator $R$, called the Gate. Fourth, if $R$ removes information required to reconstruct pre-transition identity and that information is not recoverable from the post-transition state, then irreversibility follows.

The paper contributes three things. It provides a rigorous theorem that isolates the exact assumptions needed for no-return. It strengthens the theory with additional propositions that convert the basic theorem into measurable claims: a positive lower bound on reconstruction error, monotone descent in tension under Gate updates, and a sufficient condition linking the exit ratio $\chi/C$ to eventual threshold crossing. It then anchors the theory in the user's actual computational plots, which show a repeatable regime transition across contradiction levels, and integrates a human pilot protocol that operationalizes the same architecture in cognition.

\section{Formal framework}
\subsection{State space and observables}
Let $S\neq\varnothing$ be a state space. We assume:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item a coherence functional $C:S\to\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$,
    \item a contradiction functional $\chi:S\to\mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$,
    \item a threshold constant $\theta\ge 0$.
\end{itemize}
Define the \emph{tension functional}
\begin{equation}
T(s)=\chi(s)-C(s).
\label{eq:tension}
\end{equation}
The sign of $T$ distinguishes three descriptive regimes used throughout the paper:
\begin{align*}
T(s)<0 &\quad \text{integration-dominated regime},\\
T(s)\approx 0 &\quad \text{critical regime},\\
T(s)>0 &\quad \text{contradiction-dominated regime.}
\end{align*}
The Gate threshold is then imposed at $T(s)>\theta$, which allows a nonzero margin between ordinary contradiction dominance and actual collapse.

\subsection{Identity as attractor-basin equivalence}
Let $F:S\to S$ denote ordinary admissible evolution. Identity is not defined as exact state equality but as equivalence of asymptotic organization.

\begin{definition}[Identity basin]
For $s\in S$, define its identity class
\[
I(s)=\{s'\in S: s'\sim s\},
\]
where $s'\sim s$ means that $s$ and $s'$ lie in the same attractor basin under the ordinary dynamics $F$.
\end{definition}

This definition captures the intuition that many microstates can represent the same enduring identity, while still allowing identity loss when the basin itself becomes inaccessible.

\subsection{Gate set and reorganization operator}
The Gate set is
\begin{equation}
G=\{s\in S:T(s)>\theta\}.
\label{eq:gateset}
\end{equation}
A Gate event replaces ordinary evolution by a reorganization operator.

\begin{definition}[Gate operator]
A map $R:S\to S$ is a \emph{Gate operator} if, on states in $G$, it is non-injective on identity-relevant structure and yields a post-transition state $s_+=R(s_-)$. 
\end{definition}

The theory is intentionally substrate-independent: nothing here assumes neural, physical, symbolic, or biological realization.

\subsection{Lost structure}
\begin{definition}[Identity-relevant structure]
For a pre-transition state $s_-$, let $K(s_-)$ denote the minimal information required to reconstruct the identity class $I(s_-)$. We say that $K(s_-)$ is \emph{lost} across the Gate if it is not recoverable from the post-transition state $s_+=R(s_-)$. 
\end{definition}

The role of $K$ is decisive. Irreversibility does not require that every detail of $s_-$ be lost. It requires only that enough of the structure needed to reconstruct the \emph{identity basin} is gone.

\section{The two-law architecture}
The three uploaded manuscripts contain two complementary structural laws. We unify them here in their cleanest form.

\paragraph{Law 1: existence / stability.}
A system remains within its identity-bearing regime when coherence can absorb contradiction:
\begin{equation}
C(s)\ge \chi(s).
\label{eq:law1}
\end{equation}
This is the front edge of structural viability.

\paragraph{Law 2: collapse / Gate condition.}
When contradiction exceeds coherence by more than the tolerance margin,
\begin{equation}
\chi(s)-C(s)>\theta,
\label{eq:law2}
\end{equation}
ordinary stability is lost and reorganization is forced.

\paragraph{Exit condition.}
The ``exit'' idea from the broader manuscript can be stated cleanly as a ratio law: if contradiction outgrows coherence fast enough, eventual threshold crossing follows. A theorem below makes this precise.

\section{Main theorem: point of no return}
We now state the central result in a form suitable for direct peer review.

\begin{theorem}[Point of no return]
\label{thm:pnr}
Let $s_-\in G$ and $s_+=R(s_-)$. Assume:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(A\arabic*),leftmargin=2.0em]
    \item \label{as:tension} $T(s_-)>\theta$;
    \item \label{as:noninj} $R$ is non-injective on identity-relevant structure;
    \item \label{as:lost} $K(s_-)$ is not recoverable from $s_+$;
    \item \label{as:forward} post-transition evolution proceeds only under admissible forward dynamics $F$;
    \item \label{as:identity} membership in $I(s_-)$ depends on $K(s_-)$.
\end{enumerate}
Then there does not exist any $k\in\mathbb{N}$ such that
\[
F^k(s_+)\in I(s_-).
\]
In words: no admissible forward trajectory returns the system to its pre-transition identity.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Assume for contradiction that there exists $k\in\mathbb{N}$ with $F^k(s_+)\in I(s_-)$. By \ref{as:identity}, membership in $I(s_-)$ requires the identity-relevant structure $K(s_-)$. By \ref{as:lost}, that structure is not recoverable from $s_+$. By \ref{as:forward}, the only admissible post-transition evolution is generated by repeated application of $F$, which operates solely on information available in its input states. Therefore no iterate $F^k(s_+)$ can reconstruct the missing structure $K(s_-)$. This contradicts the requirement for membership in $I(s_-)$. Hence such a $k$ cannot exist.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[No ordinary return]
\label{cor:no-return}
Under the assumptions of \cref{thm:pnr}, any return to $I(s_-)$ requires an operator $Q$ that is not generated by the admissible forward dynamics $F$.
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
If no trajectory in the semigroup generated by $F$ reaches $I(s_-)$, then any successful reconstruction must involve an operator outside that semigroup.
\end{proof}

\section{Additional mathematical support}
The main theorem establishes qualitative irreversibility. The next results convert that claim into quantitative or dynamical form.

\subsection{A positive lower bound on reconstruction error}
Assume $S$ is equipped with a metric $d$ and let
\[
\operatorname{Reach}_F(s_+)=\{F^k(s_+):k\in\mathbb{N}\cup\{0\}\}
\]
be the reachable set from the post-transition state.

\begin{definition}[Irreversibility index]
\label{def:index}
The irreversibility index of a transition $s_-\mapsto s_+$ is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{I}(s_-,s_+)=\inf_{x\in \operatorname{Reach}_F(s_+)} d\bigl(x,I(s_-)\bigr).
\label{eq:index}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Positive lower bound under basin separation]
\label{prop:positive-index}
Suppose the reachable set $\operatorname{Reach}_F(s_+)$ and the identity basin $I(s_-)$ are disjoint and have positive separation,
\[
\delta:=\inf_{x\in\operatorname{Reach}_F(s_+),\;y\in I(s_-)} d(x,y)>0.
\]
Then
\[
\mathcal{I}(s_-,s_+)\ge \delta>0.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition,
\[
d\bigl(x,I(s_-)\bigr)=\inf_{y\in I(s_-)}d(x,y)\ge \delta
\]
for every $x\in\operatorname{Reach}_F(s_+)$. Taking the infimum over all such $x$ yields \eqref{eq:index} with lower bound $\delta$.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
\cref{prop:positive-index} is the cleanest measurable support result in the paper. It shows that when the post-Gate reachable set is separated from the pre-Gate identity basin, irreversibility is not merely logical: it has a nonzero quantitative margin.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Monotonic tension descent under Gate updates}
The two-law architecture in the uploaded materials also proposes a tension-minimizing collapse rule. We formulate it here directly.

\begin{definition}[Admissible successor set]
Let $\mathcal{T}(s)$ be the set of admissible successors available from state $s$. In the collapse regime $T(s)>\theta$, define the Gate-selected successor by
\begin{equation}
s_{t+1}\in \arg\min_{s'\in \mathcal{T}(s_t)} T(s').
\label{eq:minrule}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Monotone descent in the collapse regime]
\label{prop:descent}
If $T(s_t)>\theta$ and there exists at least one admissible successor $\tilde s\in\mathcal{T}(s_t)$ with $T(\tilde s)<T(s_t)$, then any Gate-selected successor satisfying \eqref{eq:minrule} obeys
\[
T(s_{t+1})<T(s_t).
\]
In particular, Gate updates are tension-descending whenever a lower-tension admissible continuation exists.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Because $s_{t+1}$ minimizes tension over $\mathcal{T}(s_t)$ and because $\tilde s\in\mathcal{T}(s_t)$ satisfies $T(\tilde s)<T(s_t)$, we have
\[
T(s_{t+1})\le T(\tilde s)<T(s_t).
\]
\end{proof}

\subsection{Exit condition implies eventual Gate crossing}
The broader ``exit condition'' language becomes precise under mild boundedness assumptions.

\begin{proposition}[Eventual Gate crossing from exit growth]
\label{prop:exit}
Let $s(t)$ be a trajectory defined for $t\ge 0$. Assume that $C(s(t))\le M$ for all $t$ and some finite $M>0$, and that
\[
\frac{\chi(s(t))}{C(s(t))}\to \infty \qquad \text{as } t\to\infty.
\]
Then for every finite threshold $\theta$ there exists $t_\ast$ such that
\[
T(s(t))=\chi(s(t))-C(s(t))>\theta \qquad \text{for all } t\ge t_\ast.
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Because $\chi(s(t))/C(s(t))\to\infty$, there exists $t_\ast$ such that for all $t\ge t_\ast$,
\[
\frac{\chi(s(t))}{C(s(t))}>1+\frac{\theta}{M}.
\]
Since $C(s(t))\le M$, multiplying both sides gives
\[
\chi(s(t))>C(s(t))+\frac{\theta}{M}C(s(t))\ge C(s(t))+\theta\frac{C(s(t))}{M}.
\]
If additionally $C(s(t))>0$, then $\theta C(s(t))/M\ge 0$. More directly, choose $t_\ast$ so that $\chi(s(t))>(1+\theta/M)M=M+\theta\ge C(s(t))+\theta$. Hence $\chi(s(t))-C(s(t))>\theta$ for all $t\ge t_\ast$.
\end{proof}

\section{Human cognitive pilot methods integrated into the theory}
The cognitive manuscript contributes a pilot protocol rather than completed results. To avoid overstatement, we therefore include it here as a methods section for future empirical testing of the same structural architecture in human interpretation.

\subsection{Aim}
The pilot asks whether contradiction-driven reinterpretation in human subjects shows the same sequence as the computational plots: baseline coherence, contradiction accumulation, transient instability, reorganization, and reduced reversion to the original frame.

\subsection{Operational variables}
The protocol defines four observable quantities.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
    \item $\Phi$ (coherence): coder-rated structural stability of an interpretation.
    \item $\Lambda_{\mathrm{expr}}$ (contradiction expression): verbal markers of conflict, uncertainty, and self-correction.
    \item $\sigma$ (symbolic turbulence): fluctuation among competing interpretations.
    \item $I_r$ (irreversibility): difficulty returning to the initial interpretation after reorganization.
\end{itemize}
The proposed pilot composite index is
\[
I_r^{\mathrm{pilot}}=(\Phi_1-\Phi_0)+(\Lambda_{\mathrm{expr},0}-\Lambda_{\mathrm{expr},1}).
\]

\subsection{Task structure}
Participants view ambiguous low-stakes narrative scenes, provide an initial interpretation, receive sequential contradiction cues, are probed for instability, and then produce a reorganized interpretation. Finally, they are asked to revert to the original interpretation. Primary outcomes are reversion difficulty and qualitative inability to restore the original frame. The method is conceptually aligned with \cref{thm:pnr}: the human test asks whether interpretive identity is harder to reconstruct after structured contradiction-induced reorganization.

\section{Failure boundary and integrity decomposition}
The uploaded materials also motivate a decomposition of integrity into subcomponents that regulate post-Gate outcome. We treat this as a structured extension rather than a theorem, because the current evidence is qualitative.

Let
\[
\Phi=(\Phi_r,\Phi_f,\Phi_i,\Phi_m),
\]
where $\Phi_r$ is reality coupling, $\Phi_f$ is feedback tolerance, $\Phi_i$ is integration capacity, and $\Phi_m$ is constraint orientation. The intended interpretation is:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{>{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{0.28\textwidth} >{\raggedright\arraybackslash}p{0.62\textwidth}}
\toprule
Condition & Expected post-Gate behavior \\
\midrule
High $\Phi_r,\Phi_f,\Phi_i,\Phi_m$ & coherent reorganization and stable new basin \\
Low $\Phi_i$ & fragmentation; new content cannot be integrated \\
Low $\Phi_r$ and $\Phi_f$ & closed-loop distortion or delusional lock \\
Low $\Phi_m$ & destructive or unconstrained reorganization \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
This decomposition is important because it separates \emph{whether} the Gate is crossed from \emph{how} the post-Gate state stabilizes.

\section{Discussion}
The paper advances a simple but strong idea: one-way change becomes mathematically unavoidable whenever identity depends on structure that a non-injective transition removes and ordinary dynamics cannot reconstruct. This is the content of \cref{thm:pnr}. The remaining propositions are not ornamental. \Cref{prop:positive-index} shows that irreversibility can have a positive metric margin. \Cref{prop:descent} explains why collapse updates can be orderly rather than chaotic: they may descend in tension by design. \Cref{prop:exit} connects the broad philosophical notion of an ``exit condition'' to a direct, sufficient route to threshold crossing.

The empirical figures matter because they show that the theory is not floating free of data. The user's real neural plots display the most important visual signature the framework predicts: a three-regime ordering under controlled contradiction. The human pilot methods are not yet presented as results, and that honesty strengthens rather than weakens the paper. It shows exactly where the theory has already been anchored and where direct testing still needs to occur.

\section{Limitations and falsifiability}
The framework is intentionally structural, which creates both its reach and its main limitation. The theory does not specify one universal formula for coherence or contradiction; those must be operationalized per domain. The computational evidence currently supports a regime-transition picture but does not on its own estimate effect sizes or statistical significance. The cognitive portion is presently a specified pilot protocol rather than a completed dataset. Finally, cross-domain correspondences to physics should be read as structural mappings and prediction templates, not as claims that neural, cognitive, thermodynamic, and quantum systems are literally identical.

The paper remains falsifiable. It fails if one can exhibit a genuine Gate transition satisfying the assumptions of \cref{thm:pnr} and yet produce a return to the original identity basin under ordinary forward dynamics. It also fails if the predicted regime separation disappears under improved replication in the neural experiments, or if the human protocol shows easy full reversion after contradiction-driven reorganization.

\section{Conclusion}
The strongest statement supported by the merged materials is not that every irreversible phenomenon is the same, but that they can share a common structural core. The core is this: a system carries coherence and contradiction; a threshold defines the boundary of ordinary viability; a Gate event is a non-injective reorganization that removes identity-relevant structure; and once that structure is gone, admissible forward dynamics cannot restore the pre-transition identity basin.

In compact form,
\[
\boxed{\text{irreversibility} = \text{loss of recoverable identity-relevant structure under thresholded reorganization}.}
\]
The theorem-level account, the quantitative lower bound, the descent law, the exit-condition proposition, and the user's actual regime-transition plots together make the paper substantially stronger than a purely conceptual essay. What remains is empirical expansion, not conceptual rescue.

\section{Appendix A: proof details}
\subsection{Why non-injectivity matters}
A reversible update is injective on the structure needed for reconstruction. Once multiple distinct identity-relevant configurations are mapped to the same post-Gate state, exact inversion is impossible unless additional information is supplied. This is the sole mathematical content needed from ``collapse'' language; the rest of the argument depends only on missing structure and ordinary post-Gate evolution.

\subsection{Why the lower bound is stronger than the no-return theorem}
The no-return theorem establishes set exclusion: $\operatorname{Reach}_F(s_+)\cap I(s_-)=\varnothing$. The lower-bound proposition strengthens this by quantifying the exclusion. If there is positive separation between the reachable set and the original basin, then no approximation sequence under $F$ can make the reconstruction error arbitrarily small. This is the natural bridge from theorem to measurement.
